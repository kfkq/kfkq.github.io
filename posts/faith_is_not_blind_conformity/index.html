Faith is Not Blind Conformity

May 22, 2024 · 1522 words · #essay #epistemology #religion

Abstract: The modern phenomenon has already created stereotypes of what faith and religion are, often assumed to be blind conformity or merely following traditions that undermine reason. In fact, true faith only exists after certainty arises, and one cannot claim faith is valid without certainty, both in a rational and spiritual sense. Not many religions encourage knowledge, reason, and breaking away from authoritative traditions, but Islam does.

Avoid Blind Conformity

Blind conformity in belief is dangerous to one’s faith. One cannot base their faith solely on personal feelings or group customs, because as we grow up, doubt will eventually arise—not just intellectual doubt, but also doubt based on experience. A person who claims to have faith with certainty either has faith supported by necessary proof or no proof at all. It might be possible to have certainty without necessary proof due to personal experiences and feelings towards a particular group, but that certainty is at the lowest level of certainty. This is what we call typical “religious” faith, which means it is attached to particular feelings, spiritual instincts, or authority but can’t explain why that is the case. Nevertheless, I can say that for that person, that kind of certainty is still valid1, but one then cannot propagate his or her belief to other people.

But why use the term “faith” if we have proof at all? It is because, in most cases, many premises and beliefs in faith or religion are taken for granted, as they are beyond our capability to know or are simply too complicatedly subjective (regarding morality and ethics) to have rigorous proof. This makes purely abstract reasoning and spiritual instinct (let alone physical proof) impossible (if not improbable) to achieve certainty on these matters. This is where the term “necessary proofs” comes into play: one can base their belief system as long as one can justify it with necessary proofs to understand why that is the case fundamentally. For example, one can rely on scripture as long as he or she already has the necessary proof to convince themselves that this religious scripture is true2, not just in content but also historically true to its original form (authentic). In modern times, with the rise of atheism, one can easily be skeptical about the existence of God and the truthfulness of the Messenger. Then whoever has doubt on that matter, it is his or her duty to convince themselves of the truth. If one already bases their certainty on the necessary proofs of the existence of God, the truthfulness of the Messenger, and the originality and coherence of the scripture, then it is much easier to accept or at least not be easily shaken by the rest of the belief tenets of whatever religion or faith says in scripture3.

What do we mean by proof? Is it the same as how we define proof in mathematics and logic? Partly yes, due to the need for coherence and evidence that leads to a logical conclusion. But unlike in math, the evidence can be grounded in many things, not just logical statements. It can be physical, historical, or any other mode of knowing that supports it. It is sufficient for a person with intellect to argue their justification in a way that fits ordinary language and as far as their knowledge extends. There is no need to use philosophical or scientific terminology to rationally defend faith, or even to form syllogistic statements like in math. As long as it is sufficient for them to safeguard against doubt, then that is a higher degree of certainty than faith based on feelings.

A Bedouin was asked how he knew that a creator exists. He responded by saying that when he observes camel tracks and camel dung trails, he knows there is a camel. Similarly, when he observes the mountains, valleys, deserts, and oceans, he knows there is a creator. Though the Bedouin’s reasoning may seem insufficient for modern secular education, it was sufficient for him. He doesn’t have any more responsibility beyond that since his task to answer doubt or convince himself is enough based on his knowledge4.

Building rationality on necessary proofs is our first approach to having faith with strong certainty. However, it seems modern skepticism always tries to find holes in our rational inquiry5. Hence, a sound and more sophisticated response to that doubt is needed, especially if you expose yourself to many theological arguments. The responsibility to seek knowledge and answers is then necessary after doubt arises; otherwise, one cannot say the belief is valid6 if he or she is uncertain or might outwardly possess faith yet inwardly not believe, which renders them a hypocrite. Having faith and belief is acceptable and, in fact, the most useful way to attain a purposeful and meaningful life. However, what is not acceptable is ignorance and blind conformity to faith.

As a Muslim, let me briefly discuss how Islam encourages seeking certainty rather than solely submitting to authority without faith. In many instances, the Quran emphasizes the importance of knowledge and avoiding ignorance and blind conformity:

  • “Indeed, the worst of living creatures in the sight of Allah are the deaf and dumb who do not use reason.” (Quran 8:22)
  • “Ah! Ye are those who fell to disputing (even) in matters of which ye had some knowledge! But why dispute ye in matters of which ye have no knowledge? It is Allah who knows, and ye who know not!” (Quran 3:66)
  • “Nay! They say: ‘We found our fathers following a certain religion, and we do guide ourselves by their footsteps.” (Quran 43:22)

And many others. It is also an individual Muslim’s obligation (fard al-ayn) to properly learn Islamic creed (aqeedah) and a communal obligation (fard al-kifayah) to address doubts. This interestingly makes Islam itself less institutional and authoritative in essence.

The use of reason doesn’t necessitate having a sophisticated education in philosophy or science but rather being able to convince oneself regarding doubts. A person can be fully rational without being professionally trained in philosophy or rational theology. The essential requirement is to use cognitive reflection to its fullest extent, enabling one to override initial, automatic responses in favor of a more deliberate and reflective thought process.

Finally, through spiritual practice and worship, one can attain real salvation when accompanied by reason. The testimony of faith and what is said in worship only becomes genuine faith with understanding and knowledge of what is actually being uttered.
