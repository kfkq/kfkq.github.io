The Definitive and the Infinitive (النَّكِرَةُ وَ الْمَعْرِفَةُ)

Nouns in their origin always come in the infinitive form (“nakirah” in Arabic). To express the form, it carries two “dammas” (of the tanwin). E.g., رَجُلٌ (“rajulun”) meaning “a man.” It is infinitive because “a man” is unknown, and can be of infinite qualities and attributes.

But when you say “the man” in English, then, you have a particular “man” in mind, and the listener also knows ‘‘who" the man you are referring to. The two of you know who the man is. It has to be a specific man. So, ‘’the man" is a definitive noun (“ma’rifah” in Arabic). By adding an ال (“alif” and “laam”) to the noun, and remove one “harakah” out of the two “harakas”. The man becomes الرَّجُلُ.

An example differentiating the two: Nakira and Ma’rifa

  • An easy religion = دِيْنٌ يُسْرٌ

  • The easy religion = الدِّينُ الْيُسْرُ

  • The religion (of Islam) is easy = الدِّيْنُ يُسْرٌ

Al-Asmaa Ghayr al-Munsarifah

Al-Asmaa Ghayr al-Munsarifah are those nouns which never carry a “kasrah.” They will always have a ‘’fatha" no matter what the agent before them. Also, they do not come with alif laam of ma ‘rifah. Examples: لندن (London), مصر (Egypt), جهنم (Hell).
